# Obviously Awesome - Case Studies

## Successful Repositionings

### Janna Systems → CRM for Investment Banks (Big Fish, Small Pond)

Failing as "CRM for everyone." Recognized the broad CRM category had clear leaders. Repositioned narrowly: "CRM for investment banks." Result: $2M revenue → $70M in 18 months → $1.7B Siebel acquisition.

**Lesson:** Subsegmentation often beats trying to compete head-to-head with category leaders.

### Eloqua → "Marketing Automation" (Create New Game)

Originally positioned as "demand-generation automation." Mark Organ's team coined "marketing automation" to better differentiate from email automation tools. Result: $12M (2006) → $96M (2012 IPO) → $870M Oracle acquisition.

**Mark Organ on category creation:** "The most successful efforts in category creation do not result from company executives creating an acronym at an offsite. Rather they are discovered from deeply understanding a narrow set of customers. These customers are often **'freaks,' extreme in their attitudes and behavior**, forged by tectonic technological and societal shifts. The category then emerges when and if the freakish attitudes and behavior become mainstream. Category creation is hard, slow work, but if you are successful the rewards are huge."

### Wave → Free Invoicing for Solo Small Business

Found a small subset of small-business customers who LOVED the simple invoicing. Built positioning around them, ignored the rest. Grew dramatically.

**Lesson:** Best-fit customers reveal the positioning.

### Arm & Hammer → Deodorizer/Cleaner

Was sold as a baking ingredient ($16M sales). Repositioned as a deodorizer/cleaner. Sales grew to $318M by 1987. Same physical product; different positioning baggage shed.

**Lesson:** Same product can have radically different commercial outcomes under different positioning.

### Wattpad → Storytelling Platform with TV Adaptation

Wattpad started as a way for writers to create stories on the web (2006). With launch of Apple's App Store in 2009 and growth in mobile reading, repositioned around the user community of readers and writers. Eventually leveraged the audience: in 2014 Wattpad partnered with TV producers to create "Wattpad Presents" - a TV series based on popular Wattpad stories. Repositioning let them prove a new business model (story IP for TV) that wouldn't have been accessible from the original "writing platform" frame.

**Lesson:** Tracking how the audience actually uses the product reveals positioning shifts that unlock new business models.

### Redgate → Database DevOps (Trend Layer)

Database tools company layered the "DevOps" trend onto its positioning → "Database DevOps." Result: 100% increase in inbound leads.

**Lesson:** Trend layering, when the trend genuinely intersects with product strengths and customer context, amplifies positioning.

## Failure Examples

### Bic for Her (Style 3 Failure)

Tried to create "pens for women" - new category. Pens are pens. Customers ridiculed it. Product line was retired.

**Lesson:** Just because you can name a new category doesn't mean it's a real category.

### "Sharing Economy for Pets" / "Uber for Cats" (Trend Layer Failure)

Tried to layer the "sharing economy" trend onto a product that didn't actually have share-economy mechanics. Customers couldn't follow the analogy. Failed.

**Lesson:** Trend layering only works when the trend genuinely intersects with product reality.

### Long Island Iced Tea → Long Blockchain (Trend Layer Failure)

Beverage company renamed itself for the crypto trend in 2017. Got delisted from Nasdaq.

**Lesson:** Renaming for a trend without product/strategy substance is a market signal of desperation.

### "Cool Purple Lights" CIO Story (Customer Attitude Shift)

April's anecdote: at one point in the early 2000s, IT vendors put cool purple LEDs on servers because CIOs liked them in machine rooms. Within a few years, CIOs no longer cared (cloud was changing IT focus). Vendors who positioned around hardware aesthetics had baggage that stopped working.

**Lesson:** Customer attitudes shift; positioning needs check-ins.

## Counter-Example (Big Fish / Small Pond Done Right): "Coke for Dogs"

Dunford's hypothetical: take Coke and reposition for the pet-treat subsegment ("tastes like bones"). Different competitors, different value, different category - same product.

**The point:** Sometimes a subsegment IS available; you just have to look creatively. The product (Coke) is "a lot like Coke" - brown and fizzy - but does something Coke doesn't (tastes like bones, which dogs really, really want).

This is the conceptual model for Big Fish, Small Pond: don't change the product, change the segment and positioning.

## Customer Sales Story Examples

### April's Database Product (Step-Through Example)

**Context:** Database product positioned against "other databases." Sales cycles long; prospects compared to leaders like Oracle.

**Customer interviews revealed:**
- Best-fit customers were actually choosing between her product and BI tools / data warehouses
- Wrong category = wrong fight

**Reposition:**
- Step 4 (alternatives): BI tools, data warehouses
- Step 5 (attributes): real-time analytics, schema flexibility, integration depth
- Step 8 (frame): repositioned as a data warehouse with analytical capabilities
- Result: sales cycle compressed, prospects "got it" faster

**Lesson:** What customers compare you to determines whether you win.

### Appointment Scheduling Software

- First pass segmentation: "Companies with lots of locations" - too vague
- Refined: "Financial services and tax preparation" - behavioral characteristics
- Once segmentation was specific, the entire positioning clicked
