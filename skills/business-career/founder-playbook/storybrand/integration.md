# Integration - StoryBrand

## Relationship to Other Skills

### StoryBrand --> Made to Stick

**StoryBrand provides the narrative structure. Made to Stick ensures each element sticks.**

| Dimension | StoryBrand | Made to Stick |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Story structure for brand messaging | Message quality across any context |
| **Core tool** | SB7 Framework (7 story elements) | SUCCESs Checklist (6 sticky principles) |
| **Scope** | Brand-level: website, emails, campaigns | Message-level: any individual communication |

**Where they align:**
- Both fight the Curse of Knowledge (StoryBrand calls it "noise," Made to Stick calls it "the curse")
- Both demand simplicity and clarity over cleverness
- Both prioritize the audience's perspective over the communicator's
- StoryBrand's "if you confuse, you'll lose" = Made to Stick's "tappers and listeners" insight

**Where they complement:**
- StoryBrand's BrandScript gives you the WHAT to say (story structure)
- Made to Stick's SUCCESs gives you HOW to say it (sticky principles)
- Each SB7 element can be improved with SUCCESs:

| SB7 Element | Apply This SUCCESs Principle |
|---|---|
| Character (desire) | Simple - one desire, clearly stated |
| Problem (villain) | Concrete - make the villain tangible and specific |
| Guide (empathy + authority) | Credible - use anti-authorities, Sinatra Test, testable credentials |
| Plan | Concrete - specific, numbered, named steps |
| Call to Action | Simple - one primary CTA, no ambiguity |
| Failure | Emotional - individual stories, not statistics about risk |
| Success | Stories - paint the transformation as a narrative |

**Where they create tension:**
- StoryBrand says "very few words" on websites. Made to Stick says stories are essential. Resolution: use the SB7 structure for website hierarchy (few words), but tell full stories in emails, case studies, and sales conversations (where Made to Stick principles shine).
- StoryBrand focuses heavily on the customer's problem. Made to Stick warns that analytical framing (which problem descriptions can trigger) suppresses emotion. Resolution: keep problem descriptions emotional and individual, not statistical.

**Integration rule:** Design the story structure with SB7. Then run each element through the SUCCESs checklist. Is the problem Concrete? Is the guide Credible? Is the success a Story? This dual-layer approach produces messaging that is both structurally sound and individually sticky.

### StoryBrand --> Obviously Awesome

**StoryBrand tells you how to talk. Obviously Awesome tells you what to talk about.**

| Dimension | StoryBrand | Obviously Awesome |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Messaging and copy structure | Competitive positioning and category |
| **Core question** | "How do we tell our story clearly?" | "What context makes our value obvious?" |
| **Output** | BrandScript, website copy, emails | Positioning statement, competitive alternatives, category choice |

**Where they complement:**
- Obviously Awesome's 5+1 components feed directly into SB7:

| Obviously Awesome Component | Maps to SB7 Element |
|---|---|
| Competitive alternatives | Villain (what the customer uses without you) |
| Unique attributes | Guide authority (what makes you credible) |
| Value | Success (what life looks like after) |
| Target market characteristics | Character (who the hero is) |
| Market category | Context for the entire BrandScript |

- Obviously Awesome ensures you're positioned against the RIGHT competitors before you write copy
- StoryBrand ensures the positioned message is delivered in a structure that converts

**Where they create tension:**
- StoryBrand says "the customer is the hero." Obviously Awesome is more product-centric (attributes, value, category). Resolution: do the Obviously Awesome positioning work first (product-centric analysis), then translate the output into SB7 format (customer-centric narrative).
- StoryBrand de-emphasizes competitors ("don't talk about yourself"). Obviously Awesome centers on competitive context. Resolution: use competitive context to INFORM the villain and the guide's authority, but don't make the competitor the focal point of your messaging.

**Integration rule:** Position first (Obviously Awesome), then message (StoryBrand). Obviously Awesome determines WHAT you're the best at for WHOM. StoryBrand turns that into a narrative customers can follow.

### StoryBrand --> Traction

**StoryBrand is the message. Traction is the delivery mechanism.**

| Dimension | StoryBrand | Traction |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | What to say | Where to say it |
| **Core tool** | BrandScript | Bullseye Framework |
| **Metric** | Message clarity and conversion rate | CPA, channel volume, scalability |

**Where they complement:**
- StoryBrand's messaging improves every Traction channel:
  - Content marketing: blog posts follow the SB7 structure
  - Email marketing: drip campaigns use StoryBrand nurture/sales framework
  - PR: press pitches use the one-liner formula
  - SEM: ad copy addresses the internal problem (higher CTR)
  - Social ads: images show success state (not product features)

- Traction's channel testing can validate StoryBrand messaging:
  - If the same BrandScript converts across multiple channels, the messaging is strong
  - If no channel converts, the problem may be the message (StoryBrand), not the channel (Traction)

**Where they create tension:**
- StoryBrand says "very few words." Some Traction channels (content marketing, SEO) require many words. Resolution: use StoryBrand structure as the skeleton, then expand with additional content for channels that require depth.
- StoryBrand focuses on conversion. Traction includes awareness channels (PR, offline events) where conversion isn't immediate. Resolution: use the one-liner for awareness channels and the full BrandScript for conversion channels.

**Integration rule:** Build the BrandScript first (StoryBrand). Then test it through Bullseye channels (Traction). The BrandScript adapts to each channel's format while maintaining the same core story.

### StoryBrand --> $100M Offers

**StoryBrand is how you present the offer. $100M Offers is how you design the offer.**

| Dimension | StoryBrand | $100M Offers |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Messaging structure | Offer structure (value, price, packaging) |
| **Core question** | "How do we tell the story?" | "What makes the offer irresistible?" |

**Where they complement:**
- $100M Offers' value equation (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood / Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice) maps to SB7:
  - Dream Outcome = Success (Module 7)
  - Perceived Likelihood = Guide Authority (Module 3) + Plan (Module 4)
  - Time Delay = Plan specificity (Module 4)
  - Effort & Sacrifice = Plan simplicity (Module 4) + Agreement Plan (removing friction)

- A great offer (Hormozi) presented through a clear story (Miller) is the highest-converting combination
- StoryBrand's CTA section delivers $100M Offers' grand slam offer
- StoryBrand's failure section creates the urgency that $100M Offers' scarcity/urgency requires

**Integration rule:** Design the offer ($100M Offers), then tell its story (StoryBrand). The offer determines what you sell. The BrandScript determines how the customer experiences the offer in marketing.

### StoryBrand --> Mom Test

**StoryBrand tells you how to talk TO customers. Mom Test tells you how to listen TO customers.**

| Dimension | StoryBrand | Mom Test |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Outbound messaging | Inbound customer learning |
| **Direction** | You → Customer | Customer → You |

**Where they complement:**
- Mom Test conversations reveal the language for your BrandScript:
  - Customers describe their villain in their own words (use those words)
  - Customers reveal their internal problem (feelings they won't share in surveys)
  - Customers describe their desired success state (what life would look like)
  - These are direct inputs to SB7 Modules 1, 2, and 7

- Mom Test also validates your BrandScript:
  - If customers don't respond to your villain, you named the wrong one
  - If your success picture doesn't excite them, you're painting the wrong future

**Integration rule:** Listen first (Mom Test), then message (StoryBrand). Fill in the BrandScript using the customer's own words collected through Mom Test conversations. The BrandScript should sound like your customers talk, not like your marketing team writes.

### StoryBrand --> SPIN Selling

| Dimension | StoryBrand | SPIN Selling |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Marketing messaging (one-to-many) | Sales conversations (one-to-one) |
| **Problem framework** | External + Internal + Philosophical | Situation + Problem + Implication + Need-Payoff |

**Where they complement:**
- StoryBrand's three-layer problem maps to SPIN:
  - External problem = Situation/Problem questions
  - Internal problem = Implication questions (how does it make them feel?)
  - Philosophical problem = Need-Payoff questions (what would it mean to solve this?)

- Marketing (StoryBrand) generates and warms leads. Sales (SPIN) closes them. The messaging should be consistent across both.

**Integration rule:** Use StoryBrand for marketing that generates leads with a clear story. Use SPIN Selling for sales conversations that deepen the story with the individual prospect. The SB7 problem framework prepares prospects for SPIN's question sequence.

### StoryBrand --> Monetizing Innovation

| Dimension | StoryBrand | Monetizing Innovation |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Messaging | Pricing |

**Where they complement:**
- Monetizing Innovation's WTP (willingness-to-pay) research reveals how customers VALUE your offer - this directly informs StoryBrand's Success section
- If customers won't pay for your positioned value, either the pricing is wrong (Monetizing Innovation) or the messaging doesn't convey the value (StoryBrand)

**Integration rule:** Set pricing (Monetizing Innovation) and messaging (StoryBrand) together. Price signals value. Messaging explains value. They must be aligned.

### StoryBrand --> Influence

| Dimension | StoryBrand | Influence |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Narrative structure | Persuasion principles |

**Where they complement:**
- Influence's principles map to SB7:
  - Reciprocity → Transitional CTAs (give free value first)
  - Social Proof → Guide Authority (testimonials, logos)
  - Authority → Guide Authority (credentials, expertise)
  - Liking → Guide Empathy ("we understand you")
  - Scarcity → Failure section (what you lose if you don't act)
  - Commitment/Consistency → Plan (small first step leads to bigger commitment)

**Integration rule:** SB7 provides the structure. Influence principles strengthen each element within that structure.

## The Master Integration

```
WHAT TO SELL:
|-- Obviously Awesome: position against the right competitive context
|-- Monetizing Innovation: design the price alongside the product
+-- $100M Offers: package the offer to be irresistible

HOW TO SAY IT:
|-- StoryBrand: structure the message as a customer-hero narrative
|-- Made to Stick: ensure each element is Simple, Unexpected, Concrete,
|   Credible, Emotional, and told through Stories
+-- Influence: strengthen each element with persuasion principles

WHO TO SAY IT TO:
|-- Mom Test: discover who cares and what language they use
|-- Four Steps: validate the customer segment
+-- Crossing the Chasm: identify whether you're reaching visionaries or pragmatists

WHERE TO SAY IT:
|-- Traction: systematically test and select acquisition channels
+-- $100M Leads: execute within chosen channels

HOW TO CLOSE:
|-- SPIN Selling: deepen the story in 1-on-1 sales conversations
+-- Influence: leverage persuasion principles in the close
```
